# GitLaw Pro Founder Roadmap

## Decision

GitLaw Pro is the primary company bet.

Not because it is the most noble idea in the portfolio, but because it has the best combination of:

- real pain
- repeat usage
- clear buyer
- clear ROI
- strong agentic fit
- fastest path to revenue

Everything else should now be judged by one question:

`Does this help GitLaw become the default supervised legal workflow for small firms?`

If not, it is secondary.

## Founder mission

Build the safest and fastest path from intake chaos to approved legal draft for small and mid-sized European law firms.

## The company in one sentence

GitLaw Pro is the agentic legal operations layer for small firms: multilingual intake, document intelligence, verified research, draft generation, approval, and audit in one supervised workflow.

## Founder north star

`Time from intake to lawyer-approved first draft`

That is the metric the founder should care about most.

If it drops meaningfully and trust does not break, the company is getting stronger.

## Secondary company metrics

### Revenue / market

- number of paid pilots
- conversion from pilot to subscription
- monthly recurring revenue
- average revenue per firm

### Product usage

- weekly active firms
- weekly active matters
- percentage of intakes turned into structured cases
- percentage of research answers saved / approved
- percentage of drafts generated from approved research

### Workflow quality

- time from intake to structured case
- time from case to first research answer
- time from research to first draft
- percentage of document jobs completed successfully

### Trust / retention

- 30/60/90-day firm retention
- percentage of outputs reviewed and approved
- number of critical trust failures
- export / audit usage

## Founder focus rules

### Rule 1: one wedge, not all of law

For now, optimize for:

- small firms
- document-heavy work
- multilingual intake
- high-chaos practice areas

Especially:

- migration
- criminal
- family
- tenancy
- social law

Do not optimize for enterprise firms or every legal specialty at once.

### Rule 2: workflow beats cleverness

Do not ask:

- "Can the model do this?"

Ask:

- "Does this reduce time and chaos in a real matter?"

### Rule 3: trust beats flash

If there is a tradeoff between:

- more magical AI
- more reliable supervised workflow

choose the second one.

### Rule 4: document workflow is the core moat

The strongest reason to exist is not generic legal search.

It is:

`messy intake + documents + language friction + verification + drafting`

### Rule 5: every feature must land in the main loop

The main loop is:

1. intake comes in
2. files are structured
3. case is created
4. research is generated and verified
5. draft is generated
6. lawyer approves
7. export / send / archive

If a feature does not strengthen this loop, it is suspicious.

## What not to build

### Do not build broad legal encyclopedias

People do not pay for another static law portal.

### Do not build generic AI chat for lawyers

That is too horizontal and too easy to copy.

### Do not build enterprise admin complexity too early

No massive permissions matrix, no big-firm workflow abstraction before small-firm fit.

### Do not build every practice area at once

Practice-area expansion should follow proven usage, not founder curiosity.

### Do not build a "platform" first

No generic multi-agent framework as the company story.
The product is the workflow, not the orchestration engine.

### Do not overbuild marketing before pilot truth

Until firms pay and stay, product truth matters more than polished positioning.

### Do not optimize for virality

This is not a consumer app.
Depth of trust and retained firms matter more than top-of-funnel volume.

## What to build first

### Phase A: prove the wedge

Goal:

Two to three firms use GitLaw Pro on real matters and get obvious value.

Must deliver:

- intake that feels clearly better than email/WhatsApp chaos
- structured case handoff
- verified research
- draft generation that is worth reviewing
- visible onboarding and low confusion

### Phase B: make it safe enough for real work

Goal:

Firms trust it enough to keep using it.

Must deliver:

- auth
- server-side RBAC
- tenant isolation
- EU storage
- stronger audit
- session security

### Phase C: make documents the killer feature

Goal:

Files become the strongest reason to buy.

Must deliver:

- secure upload
- OCR
- translation
- classification
- original / OCR / DE / approved views

### Phase D: make the product learn per firm

Goal:

GitLaw gets better inside each practice.

Must deliver:

- approved-answer memory
- preferred wording memory
- pattern retrieval
- practice checklists

## Founder operating cadence

### Weekly

- talk to at least 3 real firms or legal professionals
- review friction in the main workflow
- review product usage events
- decide one product change that shortens the main loop

### Every 2 weeks

- ship one meaningful workflow improvement
- remove one confusing or low-value piece
- update the pilot/demo story

### Every month

- review revenue
- review retention
- review trust failures
- review whether scope is creeping

## The first 90 days

### Days 1-30

- finalize company thesis and roadmap
- define ICP and pilot offer
- create pilot pricing page / sales narrative
- close 5-10 firm conversations
- secure 2-3 design partners

Output:

- real market signal and first pilot pipeline

### Days 31-60

- finish production foundation gap map
- lock the one killer demo flow
- instrument product usage
- tighten onboarding

Output:

- product is believable as a real pilot tool

### Days 61-90

- ship secure upload + document path foundation
- sharpen the founder sales loop
- convert first design partner into paid pilot

Output:

- first revenue or very near-term revenue

## Founder sales script

Do not sell "AI for law firms."

Sell:

- less intake chaos
- less document chaos
- faster first drafts
- safer AI usage
- more leverage for small teams

The strongest story is:

`From multilingual intake and scattered files to verified research and first draft in one flow.`

## Founder decision filter

Before building anything, ask:

1. Does this strengthen the main loop?
2. Does this increase trust?
3. Would a small law firm pay sooner because of this?
4. Does this create future firm memory / stickiness?
5. Is this more important than document intelligence right now?

If 3 or more answers are "no", do not build it now.

## Ideal company trajectory

### Stage 1

Paid pilots with small firms.

### Stage 2

Repeatable pilot -> subscription conversion.

### Stage 3

Document intelligence as the wedge moat.

### Stage 4

Practice-specific agents and memory.

### Stage 5

Default operating layer for small-firm legal work in selected verticals.

## Final rule

Do not confuse momentum with progress.

Progress for GitLaw means:

- more trusted workflow depth
- more real firms
- more revenue
- more retained usage

Anything else is secondary.
